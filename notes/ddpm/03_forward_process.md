# 03. The Forward Process — Destroying the Image

## Goal of this note
Unpack every piece of notation in the sentence from your assignment:

> *"The forward diffusion process in Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) is a fixed Markov chain defined by a variance schedule $\{\beta_t\}$."*

By the end, you should be able to read that sentence and know exactly what each word means.

---

## Part 1: "Markov chain"

A **Markov chain** is a sequence of random variables $x_0, x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_T$ where the next state depends **only on the current state**, not on any earlier states. Formally:

$$
p(x_{t+1} \mid x_t, x_{t-1}, \ldots, x_0) \;=\; p(x_{t+1} \mid x_t)
$$

In plain English: *"To know what happens next, all you need is the present. The past doesn't matter."*

In DDPM, this means each noisy image $x_t$ is generated by taking the previous noisy image $x_{t-1}$ and adding a little Gaussian noise — no memory of the original image $x_0$ is needed to move from $x_{t-1}$ to $x_t$.

**Why this matters:** It makes the math tractable. The joint distribution factorizes into a product of simple transitions:

$$
q(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_T \mid x_0) \;=\; q(x_1 \mid x_0) \cdot q(x_2 \mid x_1) \cdot q(x_3 \mid x_2) \cdots q(x_T \mid x_{T-1})
$$

---

## Part 2: "Fixed"

"Fixed" means the forward process has **no learnable parameters**. We decide upfront how much noise to add at each step, and that schedule never changes during training. Contrast this with VAE/GAN encoders, which are neural networks whose weights are trained.

The only learning happens in the **reverse** process (the denoising network). The forward process is pure, deterministic math applied to random noise samples.

---

## Part 3: "Variance schedule $\{\beta_t\}$"

The variance schedule is a list of numbers $\beta_1, \beta_2, \beta_3, \ldots, \beta_T$ — one per timestep. Each $\beta_t$ tells you how much new noise to mix into the image at step $t$.

- **Small $\beta_t$ (e.g., 0.0001)**: Add only a whisker of noise. The image barely changes.
- **Large $\beta_t$ (e.g., 0.3)**: Add a lot of noise. The image changes dramatically.

The typical convention is that $\beta$ starts small and grows larger — early steps make gentle corruption, later steps make aggressive corruption. A common choice is a **linear schedule**:

$$
\beta_t \;=\; \text{linspace}(\beta_{\text{start}},\ \beta_{\text{end}},\ T)
$$

In this assignment: $\beta_{\text{start}} = 0.0001$, $\beta_{\text{end}} = 0.02$, $T = 10$.

Why "variance schedule"? Because $\beta_t$ is literally the variance of the Gaussian noise distribution used at step $t$:

$$
q(x_t \mid x_{t-1}) \;=\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(x_t;\ \sqrt{1 - \beta_t}\,x_{t-1},\ \beta_t \mathbf{I}\right)
$$

That equation says: "To get $x_t$ from $x_{t-1}$, shrink $x_{t-1}$ by a factor of $\sqrt{1 - \beta_t}$, then add Gaussian noise with variance $\beta_t$."

---

## Part 4: The auxiliary definitions

From the variance schedule, we define two more quantities for bookkeeping:

### $\alpha_t = 1 - \beta_t$

Just the complement of $\beta$. If $\beta_t$ is "how much noise we add", then $\alpha_t$ is "how much signal we keep". These always sum to 1.

Why define this? Because it makes the formulas cleaner. Instead of writing $\sqrt{1 - \beta_t}$ everywhere, we write $\sqrt{\alpha_t}$. Pure notational convenience.

### $\bar{\alpha}_t = \alpha_1 \cdot \alpha_2 \cdots \alpha_t$ (cumulative product)

This is the **product of all alphas up to step $t$**, written with a bar on top (alpha-bar, `alphas_bar` in code). It represents "how much of the original signal has survived after $t$ steps of corruption."

Example: if $\alpha_1 = 0.9$, $\alpha_2 = 0.8$, $\alpha_3 = 0.7$, then:

- $\bar{\alpha}_1 = 0.9$ &nbsp;(after 1 step, 90% of signal survives)
- $\bar{\alpha}_2 = 0.9 \times 0.8 = 0.72$ &nbsp;(after 2 steps, 72% survives)
- $\bar{\alpha}_3 = 0.9 \times 0.8 \times 0.7 = 0.504$ &nbsp;(after 3 steps, only ~50% survives)

As $t$ increases, $\bar{\alpha}_t$ shrinks toward 0 — the signal is progressively wiped out by noise.

---

## Part 5: The closed-form jump $q(x_t \mid x_0)$

This is the payoff equation of the whole note:

$$
q(x_t \mid x_0) \;=\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(x_t;\ \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\,x_0,\ (1 - \bar{\alpha}_t)\mathbf{I}\right)
$$

In plain English: **"Given the original clean image $x_0$, the noisy image at step $t$ is a Gaussian with mean $\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\,x_0$ and variance $1 - \bar{\alpha}_t$."**

### What it says intuitively

- **Mean $= \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\,x_0$**: A shrunken copy of the original image. The shrink factor $\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}$ tells you how much signal is left.
- **Variance $= 1 - \bar{\alpha}_t$**: The accumulated noise. Starts near 0 and grows toward 1 as $t$ increases.
- At $t = 0$: $\bar{\alpha}_0 = 1$, so mean $= x_0$, variance $= 0$ (you just get the original image back).
- At $t = T$: $\bar{\alpha}_T \approx 0$, so mean $\approx 0$, variance $\approx 1$ (you get pure noise).

### Why this formula is magical

The whole point is: **you don't have to walk through all $t$ steps to get $x_t$**. The math gives you a direct "teleport" from $x_0$ to any $x_t$ in one shot:

$$
x_t \;=\; \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\,x_0 \;+\; \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t}\,\varepsilon, \qquad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})
$$

This is called the **reparameterization trick** (same idea as in VAE). Instead of sampling $x_t$ from a distribution, we sample one standard Gaussian $\varepsilon$ and then transform it deterministically. This is critical for training because:

1. **Speed**: To noise an image for training, you need 1 operation, not $T$ operations.
2. **Differentiability**: The sampling step is differentiable, so gradients can flow through it.
3. **Random timestep training**: You can pick a random $t$ uniformly and directly compute $x_t$ for training, without worrying about what happened at $t-1$, $t-2$, etc.

---

## Part 6: Derivation of the closed form (for the curious)

If you want to see where $\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\,x_0$ comes from, here's the derivation. You can skim this section if you're comfortable taking the formula on faith.

### Step 1: Write one step using reparameterization

From $q(x_t \mid x_{t-1}) = \mathcal{N}(\sqrt{\alpha_t}\,x_{t-1},\ \beta_t \mathbf{I})$, using the reparameterization trick:

$$
x_t \;=\; \sqrt{\alpha_t}\,x_{t-1} \;+\; \sqrt{\beta_t}\,\varepsilon_{t-1}, \qquad \varepsilon_{t-1} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{I})
$$

Note that $\beta_t = 1 - \alpha_t$, so $\sqrt{\beta_t} = \sqrt{1 - \alpha_t}$.

### Step 2: Expand one step back

$$
x_{t-1} \;=\; \sqrt{\alpha_{t-1}}\,x_{t-2} \;+\; \sqrt{1 - \alpha_{t-1}}\,\varepsilon_{t-2}
$$

Substitute:

$$
\begin{aligned}
x_t &\;=\; \sqrt{\alpha_t}\left(\sqrt{\alpha_{t-1}}\,x_{t-2} + \sqrt{1 - \alpha_{t-1}}\,\varepsilon_{t-2}\right) + \sqrt{1 - \alpha_t}\,\varepsilon_{t-1} \\
    &\;=\; \sqrt{\alpha_t \alpha_{t-1}}\,x_{t-2} \;+\; \sqrt{\alpha_t(1 - \alpha_{t-1})}\,\varepsilon_{t-2} \;+\; \sqrt{1 - \alpha_t}\,\varepsilon_{t-1}
\end{aligned}
$$

### Step 3: Combine the two independent Gaussians

Two independent Gaussians with variances $\sigma_1^2$ and $\sigma_2^2$ sum to a single Gaussian with variance $\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2$. So:

$$
\text{Combined noise variance} \;=\; \alpha_t(1 - \alpha_{t-1}) + (1 - \alpha_t) \;=\; \alpha_t - \alpha_t \alpha_{t-1} + 1 - \alpha_t \;=\; 1 - \alpha_t \alpha_{t-1}
$$

So:

$$
x_t \;=\; \sqrt{\alpha_t \alpha_{t-1}}\,x_{t-2} \;+\; \sqrt{1 - \alpha_t \alpha_{t-1}}\,\tilde{\varepsilon}
$$

where $\tilde{\varepsilon}$ is a new standard Gaussian.

### Step 4: By induction, after unrolling all the way back to $x_0$

$$
\begin{aligned}
x_t &\;=\; \sqrt{\alpha_t \alpha_{t-1} \cdots \alpha_1}\,x_0 \;+\; \sqrt{1 - \alpha_t \alpha_{t-1} \cdots \alpha_1}\,\varepsilon \\
    &\;=\; \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\,x_0 \;+\; \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t}\,\varepsilon
\end{aligned}
$$

which matches the closed-form distribution:

$$
q(x_t \mid x_0) \;=\; \mathcal{N}\!\left(\sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\,x_0,\ (1 - \bar{\alpha}_t)\mathbf{I}\right) \qquad \blacksquare
$$

The key insight of the derivation: when you chain Gaussian-noise additions, the variances accumulate and the signal multiplies — and the cumulative product $\bar{\alpha}_t$ captures exactly this accumulation in one clean number.

---

## Takeaway

- **Forward process** = gradually add Gaussian noise via a fixed schedule
- **Markov chain** = each step only depends on the previous one
- **Fixed** = no learning here
- **$\beta_t$** = variance of noise added at step $t$ (grows with $t$)
- **$\alpha_t = 1 - \beta_t$** = fraction of signal preserved at step $t$
- **$\bar{\alpha}_t = \prod_i \alpha_i$** = cumulative fraction of original signal surviving after $t$ steps
- **Closed form** $q(x_t \mid x_0)$ lets you jump directly to any timestep in one operation
