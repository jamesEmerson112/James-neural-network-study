# Turing's Unfinished Works — What Died With Him

Alan Turing died on 7 June 1954, aged 41. He was mid-stride on at least four major
lines of research. His papers were left to Robin Gandy, who spent years trying
to piece together the fragments. Much of it remains at King's College, Cambridge,
in the Turing Digital Archive.

---

## 1. The Morphogen Theory of Phyllotaxis

**Status:** Nearly complete manuscript, unpublished until 1992

His 1952 morphogenesis paper was the *opening move*. The real prize — the one he
was chasing when he died — was **phyllotaxis**: why sunflowers, pinecones, and
daisies arrange their structures in Fibonacci spirals (1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13...).

```
    THE FIBONACCI MYSTERY IN PLANTS
    ═══════════════════════════════════════

    Sunflower seed heads:     Pinecone spirals:     Daisy florets:
    21 and 34 spirals         8 and 13 spirals      13 and 21 spirals
    (consecutive Fibonacci)   (consecutive Fib)     (consecutive Fib)

    WHY? Why do plants overwhelmingly use Fibonacci numbers?
    Turing believed reaction-diffusion could explain it.
```

He promised in the 1952 paper that "the morphogen theory of phyllotaxis" would
appear in "a later paper." He had already drafted it by the time he died.

What survived:
- A **nearly complete manuscript**: *Morphogen Theory of Phyllotaxis*
- A fragmentary companion: *Outline of the Development of the Daisy*
- Handwritten notes, diagrams, tables, and computer routines for the Manchester Mark I
- Collaboration notes with botanist **C.W. Wardlaw**

Robin Gandy wrote to Newman after Turing's death:

> *"I am particularly anxious that the work on morphogenesis on a cylinder
> should not be lost..."*

The editors of Turing's Collected Works finally published it in **1992** — 38 years
late — but struggled with the fragments. Pen-and-ink pictures Turing had drawn
were not even included.

**Modern verdict:** Turing picked an extraordinarily hard problem. Researchers
now believe he came very close to a mathematical explanation. The ideas he was
developing anticipated work that others (Jean, Adler) independently published
decades later, not knowing Turing had been there first.

## 2. The Quantum Zeno Effect ("Turing's Paradox")

**Status:** Discussed with colleagues, never formally published

Just before his death, Turing discovered something strange in quantum mechanics:

> *"If a system starts in an eigenstate of some observable, and measurements
> are made N times a second, then the probability that the system will be
> in the same state after one second tends to one as N → ∞ — that is,
> continual observations will prevent motion."*

In plain English: **watching a quantum particle freezes it in place.** The more
frequently you observe it, the less it changes. A watched quantum pot literally
never boils.

```
    THE QUANTUM ZENO EFFECT
    ═══════════════════════════════════════

    Measurement frequency:    Probability of change:
    1 per second              ~63%     (plenty of change)
    10 per second             ~10%     (mostly frozen)
    100 per second            ~1%      (nearly frozen)
    1000 per second           ~0.1%    (essentially frozen)
    ∞ (continuous)            0%       (COMPLETELY frozen)

    "A watched pot never boils" — literally true in quantum mechanics.
```

Turing and Gandy brought this to physicists, who **"rather pooh-poohed it."**

Timeline of vindication:
- **1954** — Turing discusses it with colleagues (unpublished)
- **1977** — Misra and Sudarshan formally prove it, call it "Zeno's paradox"
- **1990** — Experimentally confirmed (Itano et al.)
- **Today** — A cornerstone of quantum mechanics, sometimes called "Turing's Paradox"

Some historians believe Turing was groping toward ideas about **quantum computation**
— decades before Feynman's 1982 proposal and Shor's 1994 algorithm.

## 3. New Ideas for Fundamental Physics

**Status:** Largely lost

The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy notes that at the time of his death,
Turing had "advanced new ideas for fundamental physics" beyond the quantum
Zeno work. The details are mostly gone.

Turing was famously bad at keeping his notes. He worked things out on scraps
of paper, in his head, on blackboards. Much of what he was thinking simply
died with him.

What fragments survive suggest he was thinking about:
- The relationship between discrete and continuous systems
- How quantum mechanics relates to computation
- Whether the physical world is fundamentally computable

These are questions that **still** drive physics today (digital physics, the
simulation hypothesis, quantum information theory). Turing was asking them
in 1954.

## 4. Machine Intelligence — The Long Game

**Status:** One landmark paper (1950), clearly just the beginning

His 1950 paper *"Computing Machinery and Intelligence"* introduced:
- The Turing Test
- The concept of machine learning
- Arguments against every major objection to AI
- The idea that machines could learn from experience rather than being explicitly programmed

But he was **38** when he wrote it. It reads like a manifesto — a declaration of
what's possible — not a final statement. He clearly had decades of ideas ahead.

```
    WHAT TURING PREDICTED IN 1950
    ═══════════════════════════════════════

    "I believe that at the end of the century the use of words and
    general educated opinion will have altered so much that one will
    be able to speak of machines thinking without expecting to be
    contradicted."
                                        — slightly optimistic on timeline

    "We can only see a short distance ahead, but we can see plenty
    there that needs to be done."
                                        — his final sentence in the paper
```

He never got to pursue machine learning, neural networks, or AI beyond that
single paper. The field he founded had to wait for others to carry it forward.

## 5. Classified Cryptography Work

**Status:** Declassified 58 years late

Two papers Turing wrote during the war:
- *The Applications of Probability to Cryptography*
- *Paper on Statistics of Repetitions*

These were so valuable to GCHQ (the signals intelligence agency) that they
were **classified until 2012** — released just before the centenary of his birth.

We don't know what other cryptographic ideas he was developing after the war,
because his security clearance was revoked after his conviction. He was cut off
from the field where he'd done his most consequential work.

## Where the Papers Are

Almost everything that survived is now in the **Turing Digital Archive** at
King's College, Cambridge:

```
    THE ARCHIVE
    ═══════════════════════════════════════

    Location:       King's College, Cambridge University
    URL:            https://turingarchive.kings.cam.ac.uk/
    Provenance:     Left to Robin Gandy in Turing's will
                    Additional papers from Philip Furbank (friend/executor)

    Sections:
    AMT/A  — Publications (published papers)
    AMT/B  — Lectures and talks
    AMT/C  — UNPUBLISHED manuscripts and drafts  ← the unfinished work
    AMT/D  — Correspondence
    AMT/E  — Personal papers
    AMT/F  — Memoirs and obituaries
    AMT/K  — Photographs

    Gandy's note on the unpublished work:
    "It will be difficult, in some places impossible, to know exactly
    what the fragments are (exactly) about."
```

## The Tragedy in Numbers

```
    AGE AT DEATH:     41
    YEARS OF PRODUCTIVE RESEARCH:  ~18 (1936-1954)
    MAJOR FIELDS TOUCHED:          6+
      - Mathematical logic (Turing machine, 1936)
      - Cryptanalysis (Enigma, 1939-1945)
      - Computer architecture (ACE, 1945-1947)
      - Artificial intelligence (1950)
      - Mathematical biology (morphogenesis, 1952)
      - Quantum mechanics (Zeno effect, 1953-54)
      - Fundamental physics (fragments)

    WHAT HE PUBLISHED:             ~20 papers
    WHAT HE LEFT UNFINISHED:       at least 4 major threads
    WHAT WE'LL NEVER KNOW:         incalculable
```

His last sentence in the 1950 AI paper:

> *"We can only see a short distance ahead, but we can see plenty there
> that needs to be done."*

He saw further than anyone. He just wasn't given the time.
